## About

This hackathon is your chance to move from learning blockchain concepts to building something real.

Over the past few weeks, you have explored blockchain, Web3, Avalanche, smart contracts, wallets, and how decentralized applications can solve practical problems. Now it is time to use those ideas to create a working prototype.

The goal is not to build a perfect company in one weekend. The goal is to:

- Identify a real problem affecting people, businesses, students, communities, or institutions in Africa.
- Design a simple blockchain-based solution.
- Build a working demo or prototype.
- Explain clearly why Avalanche is useful for the solution.
- Pitch your idea with confidence.

You can build in teams. You can use open-source tools, Avalanche documentation, tutorials, starter kits, and AI assistants to help you learn faster. What matters most is that your team understands the problem, builds something functional, and can explain your work clearly.

This is a student hackathon, so judges will reward clarity, effort, learning, teamwork, and practical thinking — not just advanced code.

## Tracks

Choose a track based on the problem your team wants to solve. The best projects will not just mention Africa — they will show a real understanding of African users, constraints, and opportunities.

> **Tip:** Start with the problem, not the technology. Then ask: where does Avalanche make this better?

## Track 1: Financial Inclusion & Payments

### Theme

Build tools that make money movement, savings, payments, or financial access easier, cheaper, safer, or more transparent.

Across Africa, many people rely on mobile money, cash, informal savings groups, cross-border remittances, and small business transactions. Blockchain can help with transparency, programmable rules, faster settlement, and digital ownership.

### Example Problems

- High cost of sending money across borders.
- Lack of transparent savings group records.
- Small businesses struggling to track payments.
- Informal workers without financial history.
- Students or communities collecting funds without accountability.
- Delayed payouts for freelancers, creators, or vendors.

### Project Ideas

- A transparent digital savings group/chama platform.
- A student contribution tracker for clubs, events, or societies.
- A simple escrow app for buyer-seller transactions.
- A donation tracker for community projects.
- A cross-border remittance concept using stablecoins.
- A freelancer milestone payment prototype.
- A micro-invoice and payment proof tool for small businesses.

### How Avalanche Fits

Teams can use Avalanche to:

- Record transactions transparently.
- Build smart contracts for contribution rules.
- Create escrow logic.
- Issue digital receipts.
- Explore stablecoin-style settlement ideas.
- Build fast and low-cost dApps.

### What Judges Want To See

- A clear explanation of the financial problem.
- A simple user flow.
- A working transaction or contract demo.
- Awareness of trust, safety, and regulation.
- A realistic understanding of users in Africa.

## Track 2: Education & Credentials

### Theme

Build tools that help students, schools, training programs, or employers verify learning, achievements, certificates, and skills.

Many students in Africa face challenges proving skills, sharing certificates, or building trusted portfolios. Blockchain can help create verifiable records that are harder to fake and easier to share.

### Example Problems

- Fake certificates.
- Difficulty verifying short-course achievements.
- Students lacking proof of practical skills.
- Hackathon/project work not being recorded professionally.
- Employers struggling to verify learning history.
- Training programs needing digital certificates.

### Project Ideas

- Verifiable certificate system for course completion.
- Blockchain-based student project portfolio.
- NFT-style achievement badges for hackathon participation.
- Skill passport for students and young developers.
- Attendance and participation record system.
- Employer verification portal for student credentials.

### How Avalanche Fits

Teams can use Avalanche to:

- Issue certificates or badges.
- Store proof of achievement.
- Verify credentials publicly.
- Create student-owned digital portfolios.
- Demonstrate how credentials can be trusted without relying only on paper documents.

### What Judges Want To See

- A real education or employability problem.
- A simple credential issuing and verification flow.
- A demo showing issuer, student, and verifier roles.
- Clear explanation of what is stored on-chain vs off-chain.
- Practical design for schools, bootcamps, or universities.

## Track 3: Agriculture, Trade & Supply Chains

### Theme

Build tools that improve trust, traceability, coordination, or payments in agriculture, logistics, small trade, or supply chains.

Agriculture and trade are central to many African economies. Farmers, suppliers, transporters, buyers, and small businesses often deal with poor records, delayed payments, fake products, and weak traceability.

### Example Problems

- Farmers lacking trusted records for produce delivery.
- Buyers not knowing where goods came from.
- Fake agricultural inputs or products.
- Delayed payments between buyers and sellers.
- Poor coordination between farmers, transporters, and markets.
- Lack of transparent cooperative records.

### Project Ideas

- Farm-to-market produce tracking prototype.
- Cooperative contribution and produce record system.
- Anti-counterfeit product verification tool.
- Delivery proof system for transporters.
- Marketplace escrow for farmer-buyer transactions.
- Digital receipt system for informal traders.
- Traceability tool for coffee, tea, maize, rice, fish, or minerals.

### How Avalanche Fits

Teams can use Avalanche to:

- Record supply chain events.
- Issue proof of delivery.
- Create transparent cooperative ledgers.
- Verify product authenticity.
- Build payment or escrow logic.
- Create tamper-resistant records for trade flows.

### What Judges Want To See

- A practical trade or agriculture use case.
- A simple traceability or record-keeping demo.
- Clear roles: farmer, buyer, transporter, cooperative, consumer, etc.
- Understanding that blockchain does not automatically prove physical truth — teams should explain how data gets verified.
- Strong local relevance.

## Track 4: Public Good, Identity & Community Services

### Theme

Build tools that improve access, transparency, coordination, or accountability in communities, public services, civic activities, or identity-based systems.

Many African communities face problems around record keeping, identity, service access, public trust, and accountability. Blockchain can help when many people need to verify shared records without depending on one trusted party.

### Example Problems

- Lack of trusted community project records.
- Difficulty tracking donations or local development funds.
- Weak identity or membership systems.
- Student clubs and associations lacking transparent records.
- Community voting or decision-making needing accountability.
- Local organizations needing proof of service delivery.

### Project Ideas

- Transparent community donation tracker.
- Student association voting prototype.
- Digital membership system for clubs or cooperatives.
- Public project milestone tracker.
- NGO aid distribution proof-of-delivery tool.
- Local service request and resolution tracker.
- Identity-lite system for verified community participation.

### How Avalanche Fits

Teams can use Avalanche to:

- Record votes or governance decisions.
- Track donations and fund usage.
- Issue membership tokens or credentials.
- Create public audit trails.
- Build transparent community dashboards.

### What Judges Want To See

- Clear public or community benefit.
- Strong explanation of who uses the system.
- Careful thinking about privacy and safety.
- A simple demo showing transparency or verification.
- Avoiding unrealistic claims about replacing government systems immediately.

## Track 5: Climate, Energy & Local Impact

### Theme

Build tools that support climate resilience, clean energy, recycling, environmental monitoring, or local impact tracking.

Africa faces major challenges around energy access, climate adaptation, water, waste, and environmental change. Blockchain can help coordinate incentives, track impact, verify contributions, and improve transparency.

### Example Problems

- Poor tracking of tree planting or environmental projects.
- Lack of transparent records for recycling initiatives.
- Communities needing energy contribution/payment records.
- Difficulty verifying local climate action.
- Donors needing proof of impact.
- Students wanting to organize green campus activities.

### Project Ideas

- Tree planting verification and rewards system.
- Campus recycling rewards app.
- Solar micro-grid contribution tracker.
- Community water project donation tracker.
- Environmental impact badge system.
- Carbon-awareness education game.
- Local clean-up proof and reward platform.

### How Avalanche Fits

Teams can use Avalanche to:

- Track environmental actions.
- Issue impact badges or certificates.
- Record donations and milestones.
- Create incentive systems.
- Build transparent dashboards for community projects.

### What Judges Want To See

- A specific environmental or local impact problem.
- A simple verification method.
- A working prototype or clear demo.
- Practical local use, not vague climate buzzwords.
- Honest limits: explain what the prototype can and cannot prove.

## What Students Should Build

A strong hackathon project does not need to be huge. It should be focused.

## Recommended Scope

Your team should aim to build:

1. A simple landing page or app interface.
2. Wallet connection or mock user login.
3. One or two core smart contract functions.
4. A working demo flow.
5. A short pitch deck.
6. A GitHub repository with clear instructions.

## Good Hackathon Scope Examples

Instead of building: “A full African banking system”

Build: “A simple escrow smart contract for student marketplace payments.”

Instead of building: “A complete government identity system”

Build: “A student club membership verification prototype.”

Instead of building: “A full agriculture supply chain platform”

Build: “A product journey tracker showing farm, transporter, buyer, and consumer verification.”

Instead of building: “A national education credential platform”

Build: “A course certificate issuer and verifier for one bootcamp.”

## Submit Your Project

Follow these guidelines to submit your hackathon project successfully.

## Deadline

Submissions should close before final demos begin. Recommended: at least 1–2 hours before judging.

## Requirements

Each project must include:

1. Project name.
2. Team name and members.
3. Selected track.
4. Short description of the problem.
5. Short description of the solution.
6. GitHub repository link.
7. Pitch slides.
8. Demo link, screenshots, or video.
9. Clear instructions for running the project.
10. Explanation of how Avalanche/blockchain is used.

## GitHub README Checklist

Your README should include:

- Project title.
- Problem statement.
- Solution overview.
- Track selected.
- Features.
- Tech stack.
- Smart contract address if deployed.
- How to run locally.
- Screenshots or demo video.
- Team members.
- Future improvements.

## Pitch Deck Checklist

Your slides should include:

1. Project title and team.
2. Problem.
3. Why this matters in Africa.
4. Target users.
5. Solution.
6. Demo screenshots or flow.
7. How Avalanche/blockchain is used.
8. Impact.
9. Future roadmap.
10. Ask or closing statement.

## Evaluation Criteria

Projects will be judged on practical value, technical execution, use of Avalanche/blockchain, user experience, and pitch quality.

## Scoring Rubric

### 1. Problem & African Relevance — 20%

Judges will ask:

- Is the problem real?
- Is it relevant to students, communities, businesses, or institutions in Africa?
- Does the team understand the users?
- Is the solution grounded in local reality?

### 2. Product & User Experience — 20%

Judges will ask:

- Is the solution easy to understand?
- Can a user complete the main action?
- Is the interface clear?
- Does the demo show a real flow?

### 3. Technical Execution — 20%

Judges will ask:

- Does the prototype work?
- Is there meaningful code?
- Is the smart contract or blockchain logic functional?
- Is the project organized in GitHub?
- Did the team build something during the hackathon?

### 4. Use of Avalanche / Blockchain — 20%

Judges will ask:

- Is blockchain actually useful for this project?
- Does the project use Avalanche testnet, smart contracts, wallet connection, tokens, NFTs, or on-chain records?
- Can the team explain why Avalanche is a good fit?
- Is the blockchain use practical rather than forced?

### 5. Pitch, Teamwork & Future Potential — 20%

Judges will ask:

- Did the team explain the idea clearly?
- Was the demo convincing?
- Did team members understand their work?
- Is there a realistic next step after the hackathon?
- Could this become a student project, startup idea, campus tool, NGO tool, or community solution?

---

## Judging Guidance

Judges should remember that this is a student hackathon.

Reward teams that:

- Chose a real problem.
- Built a working prototype.
- Learned quickly.
- Used Avalanche meaningfully.
- Explained their idea clearly.
- Thought seriously about African users.

Do not reward projects only because they sound big. A small working solution is better than a huge idea with no demo.

---

## Rules

1. Teams should build during the hackathon period.
2. Existing open-source code and tutorials may be used, but teams must explain what they changed or built.
3. Projects should use Avalanche or clearly explain how Avalanche would be used.
4. Teams must submit before the deadline.
5. Teams must include a GitHub repository.
6. Teams must present their own work.
7. Plagiarism is not allowed.
8. AI tools may be used for learning, debugging, and drafting, but teams must understand and explain their project.
9. Judges’ decisions are final.
10. Projects should be respectful, legal, and safe.

---

## Mentor Guide

Mentors should help teams by asking practical questions:

- What problem are you solving?
- Who exactly is the user?
- Why does this matter in Africa?
- What is the smallest demo you can build?
- Where does blockchain add value?
- What can you remove to make the project simpler?
- What must work by demo time?
- Can you explain this in one minute?

Mentors should encourage students to reduce scope and focus on a working demo.

## How To Choose A Good Idea

Use this test:

1. Can we explain the problem in one sentence?
2. Do we know who the user is?
3. Can we demo the main action in under two minutes?
4. Does blockchain make the solution better?
5. Can we build a simple version before the deadline?
6. Is the idea relevant to Africa?

If the answer is yes, it is probably a good hackathon idea.

---

## Final Advice To Students

Build something small, useful, and clear.

Do not try to solve every problem at once. The best hackathon projects usually do one thing well.

A strong project is not just code. It is a combination of:

- A real problem.
- A focused solution.
- A working demo.
- Clear explanation.
- Good teamwork.
- Practical use of technology.

This hackathon is not only about winning $1,000. It is about learning how to build, pitch, collaborate, and think like problem-solvers.

Africa has many real challenges — payments, education, agriculture, trade, identity, public trust, climate, and opportunity. Use this hackathon to build something that points toward a better future.

Build boldly. Keep it simple. Demo clearly.
